Is Te Tiriti Still Relevant in Today's NZ?

Feb 03, 2025 • By Breviss Wolfgramm

Is Te Tiriti Still Relevant in Today's NZ?

Te Tiriti o Waitangi, signed in 1840, is widely regarded as New Zealand's founding document and continues to serve as a living, dynamic agreement that underpins the relationship between Māori and the Crown. Far from being a relic of the past, Te Tiriti remains central to shaping modern Aotearoa, influencing social, political, and cultural landscapes while serving as a framework for justice and partnership. Its principles, rooted in partnership, participation, and protection, provide a guiding vision for fostering equity and upholding the rights of Māori as tangata whenua.

The significance of Te Tiriti extends well beyond the historical moment of its signing. As highlighted by the Royal Australian and New Zealand College of Psychiatrists, Te Tiriti is foundational to shaping New Zealand\'s social and health policies. It places clear responsibilities on the government and organizations to actively protect and promote the health and well-being of Māori communities.

This includes addressing systemic inequities, ensuring equitable access to services, and creating structures that reflect the values and priorities of Māori. In doing so, Te Tiriti not only informs policy but also drives action toward a fairer, more inclusive society that recognizes and values the unique contributions of Māori culture and identity.

Role of Tino Rangatiratanga

Tino rangatiratanga, a fundamental concept in Te Tiriti o Waitangi, refers to Māori self-determination, sovereignty, and autonomy over their resources, culture, and communities.

This principle is central to the modern interpretation of the Treaty relationship, emphasizing Māori control over their affairs and the protection of their interests. The concept extends beyond mere consultation, requiring active participation of Māori in decision-making processes that affect their lives.

In practice, tino rangatiratanga challenges the public sector to develop new partnership arrangements with Māori. This includes co-designing services that recognize differing Māori needs, striving for equity of outcomes, and providing options for Māori to participate in ways appropriate to their circumstances.

The implementation of tino rangatiratanga aims to create a more balanced relationship between Māori and the Crown, moving towards a model where Māori have greater control over their resources and destinies while working alongside the government in a true partnership.

Modern Interpretations of Partnership

Modern interpretations of Te Tiriti o Waitangi emphasize a partnership between Māori and the Crown based on mutual respect and good faith. The concept of partnership has evolved to recognize the need for meaningful engagement and power-sharing in decision-making processes.

According to the National Iwi Chairs Forum, there is a growing call for mature conversations about how rangatiratanga (Māori self-determination) and kāwanatanga (government) can work side by side in practice.

This interpretation challenges the historical imbalance of power and seeks to create a more equitable relationship between Treaty partners.The partnership principle has practical implications for governance and policy-making.

Government institutions are increasingly expected to engage with Māori in ways that enable them to exercise tino rangatiratanga over their own health and wellbeing, and to contribute to equitable outcomes for Māori.

This approach requires Crown-funded organizations to not only consult with Māori but to actively involve them in decision-making processes, recognizing the value of Māori knowledge and perspectives in addressing societal challenges.

Equity in Health Services

The New Zealand health system recognizes the need for equitable health services for Māori, acknowledging historical disparities and the importance of culturally appropriate care.

To address this, the government has developed frameworks and initiatives focused on achieving health equity for Māori through a Te Tiriti o Waitangi-based approach.

Key strategies include developing kaupapa Māori health services, which are designed by Māori, for Māori, underpinned by tikanga and delivered by providers who identify as Māori.

These services aim to improve access, cultural responsiveness, and health outcomes for Māori communities.

●        The Equity of Health Care for Māori Framework guides health practitioners, organizations, and the system to achieve equitable healthcare through leadership, knowledge, and commitment.

●        Initiatives like the Access and Choice programme offer free, culturally relevant wellbeing services as early interventions for Māori.

●        Health organizations are increasingly required to analyze performance data by ethnicity and other factors to track progress in achieving health equity for Māori.

●        There is a growing emphasis on Māori-led, regionally-based approaches that embrace local mātauranga (knowledge) to meet specific needs and improve experiences within the health system.

Addressing Systemic Health Disparities

Addressing systemic health disparities for Māori requires a multifaceted approach that goes beyond traditional healthcare models. Research has consistently shown that rangatahi Māori face poorer health outcomes compared to Pākehā youth, with little improvement over the past two decades despite government efforts.

To combat these inequities, there is a growing call to indigenize the health system by placing the rights, needs, and preferences of Māori youth and their families at its core.

Key strategies to address these disparities include:

●        Developing Māori-led health initiatives that incorporate mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge) to enhance connection to te ao Māori and improve health outcomes.

●        Implementing cultural safety practices and anti-racist policies throughout the health system to combat institutionalized racism.

●        Reconfiguring services to deliver high-quality, culturally appropriate care that meets the specific needs of Māori communities.

●        Investing in the development of organizational health equity expertise and requiring performance data to be analyzed by ethnicity to track progress.

●        Creating frameworks that protect the rights of individuals and whānau using health services, ensuring equitable access and quality of care.

By focusing on these areas, the health system can work towards fulfilling its obligations under Te Tiriti o Waitangi and create a more equitable healthcare environment for Māori.

Environmental Co-Governance Models

Environmental co-governance models in Aotearoa New Zealand represent a significant shift towards shared decision-making between Māori and the Crown in managing natural resources.

These arrangements, often arising from Treaty of Waitangi settlements, aim to integrate diverse environmental values and interests of local communities with state and non-state actors.

The Waikato River Authority exemplifies this approach, featuring a ten-member board with equal representation from Māori iwi and Crown appointees.

Key aspects of environmental co-governance include:

●        Recognition of mātauranga and tikanga Māori in ecosystem-based management approaches.

●        Incorporation of Māori constitutional relationships and mana whenua at all levels (whānau, hapū, iwi).

●        Focus on long-term sustainability and maintaining values for future generations.

●        Collaborative processes involving agencies, iwi, industries, and local communities.

●        Integration of kaitiaki (guardianship) interests in environmental decision-making.

These models aim to fulfill Treaty obligations while addressing complex environmental challenges, potentially positioning New Zealand as a leader in sustainable resource management that respects indigenous rights and knowledge.

Education and Bicultural Collaboration

Te Tiriti o Waitangi has significantly shaped New Zealand\'s education system, emphasizing bicultural collaboration and the integration of Māori language and culture.

The Education and Training Act 2020 requires school boards to give effect to Te Tiriti, ensuring that Māori perspectives and knowledge are valued in educational settings.

This commitment is reflected in initiatives like Te Whāriki, the early childhood curriculum, which affirms that all children should develop an understanding of New Zealand\'s bicultural heritage.

Key aspects of bicultural education in New Zealand include:

●        Providing opportunities for all students to learn te reo Māori and tikanga Māori.

●        Incorporating mātauranga Māori (Māori knowledge) into teaching practices.

●        Developing partnerships between schools and local iwi to create culturally inclusive content.

●        Investing in infrastructure and learning support for kaupapa Māori and Māori medium education.

●        Training teachers to demonstrate commitment to the bicultural partnership and to work effectively in bicultural contexts.

These efforts aim to address historical inequities and create an education system that honors Te Tiriti o Waitangi while preparing all students for life in a bicultural society.

Economic Empowerment Strategies

The Māori economy has seen significant growth and development in recent years, driven by strategic initiatives and partnerships.

The Provincial Growth Fund (PGF) has played a crucial role in supporting Māori economic development projects, including land development, regional initiatives, and business investments.

Key strategies for economic empowerment include:

●        Allocating \$100 million from the PGF to support Whenua Māori projects, addressing barriers to capital funding for Māori landowners.

●        Investing in Māori-led businesses, such as mussel spat hatcheries and processing facilities, creating employment opportunities in regions with high Māori populations.

●        Developing infrastructure in Māori communities, including upgrades to marae facilities.

●        Focusing on high-value sectors like construction, infrastructure, and primary industries to scale up Māori and Pasifika business operations.

●        Implementing procurement policies to increase access for Māori businesses to government contract opportunities.

These initiatives aim to stimulate economic growth, create jobs, and enhance wellbeing for Māori, aligning with the broader goal of increasing Māori contribution to the national economy.

Conclusion

In conclusion, Te Tiriti o Waitangi remains an essential cornerstone of Aotearoa New Zealand, transcending its historical origins to influence modern society in profound ways. Its principles of partnership, participation, and protection are as relevant today as they were in 1840, guiding efforts toward equity, justice, and meaningful collaboration between Māori and the Crown.

Whether through health equity initiatives, environmental co-governance, education reforms, or economic empowerment strategies, Te Tiriti serves as a living document that shapes policies, practices, and partnerships. It challenges institutions to honor their obligations and to foster a society that genuinely reflects the bicultural foundation of New Zealand.

As Aotearoa continues to navigate its unique path, the relevance of Te Tiriti lies not only in recognizing past injustices but also in forging a future grounded in mutual respect, shared responsibility, and the celebration of Māori culture and identity. Its enduring significance is a testament to its power as a framework for a more inclusive, equitable, and united nation.
