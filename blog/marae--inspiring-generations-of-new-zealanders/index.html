Marae: Inspiring Generations of New Zealanders

Feb 18, 2025 • By Breviss Wolfgramm

Marae: Inspiring Generations of New Zealanders

Marae are more than just meeting places; they are the spiritual and cultural heartbeat of Māori communities, carrying the wisdom of generations and shaping the identity of future ones.

As living repositories of tradition, language, and values, marae serve as spaces where storytelling, education, and cultural practices flourish.

From the preservation of oral histories to the revitalization of Te Reo Māori, these sacred sites remain essential in fostering a deep sense of belonging and continuity for young Maori in an ever-evolving world.

By understanding the profound role of marae, we can appreciate their significance not only as cultural landmarks but also as dynamic centers of learning, resilience, and connection.

Preserving Legends for Future Generations 

Preserving Māori legends for future generations is a crucial aspect of maintaining cultural identity and heritage. Pūrākau, or traditional Māori stories, are structured narratives with a beginning, middle, and end that serve as vehicles for transmitting cultural values, lessons, and history.

These stories are more than mere entertainment; they explain the past, remember experiences, and pass on learning to younger generations.

To ensure the continuity of these oral traditions, sustainable tourism initiatives have emerged as a powerful tool for preservation. By supporting community-driven projects that safeguard storytelling traditions, sustainable tourism enables local customs to thrive in the modern world.

For instance, eco-tourism models work with Indigenous communities to host travelers in ways that incorporate authentic cultural experiences, introducing visitors to legends tied to specific landscapes or historical landmarks.

This approach not only preserves the integrity of cultural sites but also reinforces the role of folklore in maintaining community identity, ensuring that these valuable tales remain alive and relevant in an increasingly globalized era.

Oral Histories and Ancestral Wisdom

Māori oral tradition serves as a rich repository of cultural heritage, encompassing whakapapa (genealogies), whakataukī (sayings), kōrero (narratives), and waiata (songs and chants).

These oral histories not only preserve the worldview and values of the Māori people but also convey profound truths about the relationship between humans, nature, and the spiritual realm.

For instance, the tale of Tāne Mahuta\'s separation of Ranginui and Papatūānuku illustrates the interconnectedness of all living things.

Pūrākau (mythological traditions) are statements about the nature of the world, and their recitation ritually \'recreates\' the world.\

Storytelling often occurs in marae, where communities gather to listen and learn.

These narratives provide guidance on ethical conduct, environmental stewardship, and interpersonal relationships.\

The concept of tei laititi-tei matua, similar to tuakana-teina, involves older children teaching younger ones, ensuring the continuity of cultural knowledge.

Role of Marae in Education 

Marae have emerged as transformative spaces in education, particularly for Māori learners, by fostering cultural identity and academic success.

School-based marae, or marae-ā-kura, integrate te reo Māori, tikanga, and kawa into daily learning, creating an environment where students can live and learn as Māori within mainstream schools.

These spaces provide a culturally safe setting for students to engage in practices like pōwhiri and whaikōrero, which might not be accessible at their ancestral marae. Beyond schools, marae-based education for adults has proven effective in enhancing literacy and language skills through culturally grounded programmes.

By prioritizing te reo Māori and incorporating spiritual and ancestral dimensions, these initiatives have significantly improved participants\' language proficiency and cultural confidence. Marae thus act as powerful educational hubs that bridge cultural heritage with contemporary learning needs.

Language Revitalization Initiatives

Te Reo Māori revitalization efforts have gained significant momentum through various initiatives aimed at preserving and promoting the language. Kōhanga Reo (language nests) and Kura Kaupapa Māori (Māori-medium schools) have been instrumental in creating immersive environments for young learners, ensuring intergenerational transmission of the language.

These educational initiatives, established in the 1980s, have played a crucial role in addressing the long-standing pattern of Māori language loss resulting from British colonization.

Community-led efforts have also been pivotal in the revitalization process. Volunteers and activists have organized language classes, workshops, and mentoring programs to provide accessible opportunities for people of all ages to learn and practice Te Reo Māori.

Additionally, the development of resources like Poutama Reo, a quality framework for Te Reo Māori in English-medium schools, demonstrates a collaborative approach between government agencies, educational institutions, and Māori communities to support language revitalization efforts.

These initiatives collectively contribute to raising the status of Te Reo Māori, promoting its learning, and creating opportunities for its everyday use in various contexts.

Cultural Practices in Marae Life

Marae life is steeped in rich cultural practices that form the backbone of Māori traditions and social interactions. At the heart of these practices is the pōwhiri, a formal welcoming ceremony that removes the tapu (sacredness) from visitors, known as waewae tapu or sacred feet.

This ceremony integrates various elements of Māori culture, including oratory, language, and social etiquette, which are given their fullest expression on the marae.

The communal nature of marae life is evident in shared activities and spaces. During important occasions, people sleep together in the wharenui (meeting house), eat in the wharekai (dining hall), and collectively participate in chores and tribal discussions.

This communal living reinforces the concept of whanaungatanga (kinship) and provides a unique environment for learning, debating, and celebrating Māori culture.

The marae also serves as a venue for significant life events such as tangihanga (funerals), hui (meetings), and educational workshops, ensuring the continuity of Māori customs and values across generations.

Community Anchor Points

Marae serve as the haukainga (home people) for Māori communities, providing a vital hub for cultural, social, and economic activities. These sacred spaces act as anchors for Māori identity, offering a tūrangawaewae (place to stand) for both urban and rural Māori.

The role of marae extends beyond traditional gatherings, evolving to meet contemporary community needs:

  • Emergency response: During crises like the COVID-19 pandemic, marae distributed supplies and provided testing and vaccination facilities for Māori and the wider community.
  • Social services: Many marae offer health, education, and social services, including kōhanga reo early childhood education.
  • Cultural preservation: Marae host educational workshops and serve as spaces where traditional Māori oratory, language, and social etiquette are fully expressed.
  • Community resilience: As focal points for Māori leadership, marae play an adaptive role in addressing challenges faced by their communities.\

The evolving nature of marae as community centers necessitates ongoing support for their governance, management, and infrastructure to ensure they continue to serve as the beating heart of Māori culture and community well-being.

Intergenerational Knowledge Through Storytelling

Storytelling plays a crucial role in transmitting intergenerational knowledge and fostering cultural continuity within Māori communities. Passed down orally through whānau (family) and iwi (tribal) networks, these stories serve as anchors of cultural identity, reinforcing connections to the land, ancestors, and collective memory of the Māori people.

Through pūrākau (myths), korero (tales), whakataukī (proverbs), and waiata (songs), Māori elders impart traditional wisdom, values, and tikanga (customs) to younger generations.

  • Storytelling often takes place in marae, traditional meeting places, where communities gather to listen, watch, and learn.
  • The concept of whakapapa (genealogy) is central to Māori storytelling, tracing connections between people, places, and things.
  • These narratives provide a framework for understanding the world, offering guidance on ethical conduct, environmental stewardship, and interpersonal relationships.
  • Modern interpretations of these timeless tales continue to evolve, addressing contemporary issues while preserving cultural heritage.\

Conclusion

Marae continue to stand as pillars of Māori identity, bridging the past, present, and future through storytelling, education, and communal life.

They are not just places of tradition but evolving hubs that adapt to modern challenges while preserving cultural integrity.

Whether through the transmission of ancestral wisdom, the revitalization of language, or their role in social services, marae remain vital in strengthening Māori communities and inspiring new generations.

As guardians of heritage and agents of change, marae will undoubtedly continue to play an essential role in shaping Aotearoa's cultural landscape for years to come.
